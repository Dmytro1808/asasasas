Explore the Magical Wild Atlantic Way
By bus
Discover the 2,500km of scenic coastline with white sands, towering sea cliffs and unique local culture to get Wild Atlantic Way certificate. Feel the power of the Atlantic Ocean as it crashes into the base of sea cliffs, take a stroll along vast beaches and watch flocks of birds glide in the skies above.
We will start our journey with a visit to the
Inishowen peninsula
The Inishowen Peninsula includes the most northern point of Ireland, Malin Head. On the territory of the peninsula, numerous archaeological and historical monuments have been preserved, including the ruins of several castles and the Grianan of Aileach fort (the 'Solar Temple of Aileach').
Fanad Head
Donegal
Hike to the top of the Sliabh Liag (Slieve League) cliffs on the south west coast of County Donegal and marvel at the terrific views of the Atlantic Ocean, the Sligo Mountains and Donegal Bay. And we will available to see Fanad Head. Fanad Head is one of three Signature Discovery Points on the Wild Atlantic Way in Donegal. Fanad Lighthouse has been a working lighthouse since 1817.
Donegal Bay and Sligo
Sligo
Strandhill is a lively seaside village and holiday resort on the Coolera peninsula in Co. Sligo. Just 7km from Sligo town and at the foot of Knocknarea mountain, it offers one of Europe's best surfing spots. The beachfront is the village's vibrant hub, with excellent restaurants, pubs, and stunning Irish sunsets in front of the iconic cannon.
Erris
Natural wonders of the Wild Atlantic Way
One of the most impressive natural wonders of the Wild Atlantic Way is located just a stone's throw from the coast of Downpatrick Head. Here, the sea stack Dún Briste rises around 50 metres high from the waves and defies the forces of the sea. The stretch of coast between Downpatrick Head and Benwee Head is lined with several cliffs.
Achill Island and Clew Bay
Pirate country
This section of the Wild Atlantic Way leads through "Pirate country" along the shores of the pirate queen Granuaile, also known as Grace O'Malley. There are two tower houses of the legendary buccaneer along the route: Carraigahowley Castle (also known as Rockfleet Castle) and Kildavnet Castle on Achill Island.
Connemara
Twelve Pins and Maamturk Mountains
On narrow coastal roads near the Twelve Pins and Maamturk Mountains, this Wild Atlantic Way section traverses Connemara, the wild heart of Ireland. The region is shaped by the Atlantic Ocean, its waves, currents, and winds.
The Burren and West Clare
Cliffs of Moher
The first part of this section of the Wild Atlantic Way appears to be out of this world. The reason is the mesmerising karst landscape of the Burren, a brittle limestone mountain range with a rugged and rocky surface. The translation of the Irish name "Boirinn" is "stony place" and it could hardly be more accurate. The Cliffs of Moher is the most famous tourist attraction on the west coast of Ireland. The cliffs rise up to 214 metres out of the Atlantic.
The Shannon Estuary
Bridges of Ross
In the heart of West Clare, not too far from Kilkee, you will find a pristine coastal landscape with dramatic cliffs. Here, the Loop Head Drive runs along the rocky coastline - just a few metres off the cliff edge. The impressive rock formations rise up to a height of 80 metres against the waves of the Atlantic. The Bridges of Ross were originally three rock arches that spanned deep coastal incisions like bridges. Nowadays there is only one of these “bridges” – the other two droped to the sea.
Dingle Peninsula
Castlegregory
The Dingle Peninsula is blessed with never ending sandy beaches and dune formations both to the north (near Castlegregory) and to the south (near Inch).
Castlegregory is located in the north of the Dingle Peninsula on the Maharees Peninsula. This headland is flanked by miles of sandy beaches that stretch west into Brandon Bay and east into Tralee Bay.
Ring of Kerry
Castlegregory
This section of the Wild Atlantic Way is largely overlapping with the famous Ring of Kerry. The "Ring" is the most frequently travelled tourist route in Ireland to date and follows the coastline of the Iveragh Peninsula.
The Cahergal Stone Fort is an impressive example of an Iron Age ring fort. A few of such forts can be found in Ireland. The restored fort has a massive outer wall measuring 4 metres at the highest point. Inside the ring fort are the remains of a circular building.
Beara and Sheep’s Head
Bantry House and Gardens
We are entering the final part of our journey to see The Ring of Beara. Less busy than the Ring of Kerry, its narrow roads are unsuitable for buses and motorhomes. This section of the Wild Atlantic Way on the Beara Peninsula leads to quiet, sparsely populated areas. The rugged coastline and rocky mountains are constantly shaped by the Atlantic.
West Cork
Baltimore Beacon
We finish our incredible route to West Cork. We will see Mizen Peninsula in the south-west of Ireland is a true hidden gem. It combines all the special characteristics that make the Irish west coast so charming: beautiful sandy beaches, dramatic cliffs and picturesque fishing villages.
The Baltimore Beacon, a whitewashed tower guarding the entrance of the harbour, is Baltimore’s major landmark. The locals call him Lot’s Wife with a wink after a biblical figure that solidified into a pillar of salt.
